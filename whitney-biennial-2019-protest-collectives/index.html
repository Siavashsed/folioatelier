When Art Collectives Walked Out of the Whitney in 2019

The 2019 Whitney Biennial protests reshaped how art collectives think about institutional power. Here's what actually happened and why it still matters.

Forty-seven artists. One board member with ties to tear gas manufacturing. And a protest action that forced the art world to ask, out loud, whether a museum's donor list is a moral document. The 2019 Whitney Biennial became the most talked-about institutional crisis in American art that year, and I watched a significant chunk of it unfold from the floor of the Meatpacking District building during opening week in May. I'd gone to cover the show for a piece on emerging collectives, notebook in hand, and ended up spending most of my time not looking at the walls but watching artists argue in the lobby about whether to pull their work entirely. One painter I spoke with, someone who'd waited years for a Whitney placement, told me she'd made her decision at 11pm the night before. She stayed in. She's still not sure it was the right call.

What made the 2019 Biennial different from earlier institutional controversies wasn't just the scale of the response. It was the organized, collective nature of it. This wasn't a single artist writing an op-ed. It was groups, specifically groups, coordinating withdrawals and public letters. That shift, from individual dissent to collective action inside a major institution, is the thing worth tracking if you care about where artist collectives are headed.

Warren Kanders, Safariland, and the Specific Complaint

Warren Kanders had been vice chairman of the Whitney's board since 2013. His company, Safariland, manufactures crowd-control equipment including tear gas canisters. In November 2018, The New York Times reported that Safariland products had been used at the US-Mexico border against migrants. By the time the 2019 Biennial opened in May, that reporting had aged into something that felt impossible to ignore standing inside a building with his name attached to its funding.

The coalition that formed around the Kanders issue included Decolonize This Place, a New York-based art and activist collective that had been running regular actions inside the museum since 2018. They organized eight consecutive weeks of protests, every Wednesday, inside the Whitney. That's not a social media campaign. That's physical, sustained, organized presence, and it drew real crowds. Hyperallergic documented the withdrawals closely, and by July, eight artists had removed their work from the show. Kanders resigned from the board on July 25, 2019.

The specific complaint mattered. This wasn't abstract. Protesters brought actual Safariland canisters to actions. Artist Forensic Architecture, one of the collectives in the show, presented a video piece called Triple-Chaser that used machine learning to track Safariland's tear gas grenades across conflict zones globally. The work was, in effect, an indictment of the institution hosting it. That kind of structural irony doesn't happen by accident. It was engineered.

How Collectives Organized Differently Than Individual Artists

The distinction between solo artists and collectives in this situation was visible and real. Individual artists in the Biennial made personal, often agonizing choices. Collectives moved differently. They had internal decision-making processes, shared public statements, and didn't have to absorb the full reputational cost alone. There's genuine strength in that structure when an institution applies pressure. For more on this, see How the Whitney Biennial 2022 Forced a Real Conversation About Collective Power in American Art.

I've seen this pattern before in smaller contexts, regional biennials and residency programs where a single artist pulling out creates drama, gets labeled difficult, and sometimes loses future opportunities. A collective pulling out is a statement. The asymmetry is not subtle. When eight entities withdraw rather than eight individuals, the story is different. The museum's PR calculus is different. The coverage is different.

According to a Grantmakers in the Arts 2019 survey, artist-led collectives represented around 23% of applicants to major US arts funding pools that year, up from roughly 15% in 2015. That growth isn't incidental. Collectives were already gaining institutional traction before the Whitney crisis. The Biennial events accelerated a conversation that was already happening about what collective membership actually protects.

There's a misconception worth addressing here. A lot of commentary framed the 2019 withdrawals as anti-institutional radicalism, as if the artists involved wanted to burn the whole system down. Most didn't. Most wanted a seat at the table. The withdrawal was a negotiating tool, not a rejection of museums as a concept. That distinction matters for understanding what collectives actually want from institutions.

What the Art World Got Wrong in Its First Reaction

The initial response from a portion of the art establishment was, charitably, confused. Several prominent critics and curators argued that mixing ethics with aesthetics was a category error, that the art should be judged on its own terms, that institutional funding has always been complicated. That position aged badly by August. It also missed the actual argument being made.

The protesters were not claiming the art was bad. They were claiming the institutional frame corrupted the conditions under which the art existed. That's a different philosophical claim, and it's one with a real history in arts criticism going back at least to Hans Haacke's work in the 1970s. When Haacke's Shapolsky et al. Manhattan Real Estate Holdings piece got pulled from the Guggenheim in 1971, the institutional response was similar: this isn't the place for this conversation. Fifty years later, the conversation happened anyway. For more on this, see How the Sudden Pivot to Virtual Art Fairs in 2020 Reshaped the Collectives Scene Forever.

Okay, I'll be honest about something slightly embarrassing here. When I first read the letter from the withdrawing artists in late June, my immediate reaction was that it was too long and too procedural to land with general audiences. I said this to a colleague over coffee and she looked at me like I'd said something genuinely stupid. She was right. The letter wasn't written for general audiences. It was written for the institution. It needed to be procedural. I'd confused the intended reader. The thing is, I've made that mistake before with collective communications, assuming they're broadcasting when they're actually negotiating.

The broader art press also underestimated how much the crisis would influence collector and donor behavior going forward. By late 2019, several mid-size institutions had quietly started reviewing board member affiliations more carefully. Not loudly. Not with press releases. But the conversations were happening.

The Longer Signal for Artist Collectives Everywhere

The Whitney events in 2019 sent a clear signal to collectives operating outside New York: institutional participation comes with institutional entanglement, and you need to know your own lines before you're standing in a lobby at 11pm trying to figure them out. That sounds obvious. In practice, most collectives don't have that conversation until a crisis forces it.

In my work with smaller regional collectives over the past several years, the groups that handled funding controversies best were the ones with something like a written ethics framework, even a rough one, agreed on before the money showed up. The 2019 Whitney situation gave collectives everywhere a concrete case study to point to. Before 2019, these conversations were theoretical. After July 2019, they were practical.

The downstream effects are already visible. The National Endowment for the Arts saw an uptick in applications specifically from collectives in 2019 and 2020, partly because collectives were formalizing their structures in response to moments like this, which made them eligible for grant categories previously dominated by 501(c)(3) nonprofits. That formalization has real costs and real benefits, and the debate about whether collectives should incorporate is ongoing. For more on how that played out financially, the NEA's 2020 CARES Act funding became its own flashpoint for exactly these tensions a year later. For more on this, see How the Forced Sale of Dia Art Foundation's Beacon Campus Shook the Collectives World in 2023.

And here's the thing... the Kanders resignation didn't resolve anything fundamental. The structural question, who funds art, under what conditions, with what transparency, remains completely open. The Whitney situation made it visible. Visibility isn't resolution. But for collectives building their practices in 2019, visibility was a start. The groups that understood that distinction went into 2020 better prepared than the ones that treated July 25th as a clean ending. It wasn't. It was the beginning of a longer argument, one that the 2022 Whitney Biennial would pick back up with different stakes and a different cast.

If you're part of a collective right now, do one thing before your next institutional opportunity lands: get your ethics framework on paper, even two paragraphs, before the invitation arrives. The 2019 Whitney situation showed that the artists who handled it with the most clarity were the ones who'd already had the hard internal conversation. The ones still having it in public, in real time, under institutional pressure, that's a much harder place to think straight.

Frequently asked questions

Why did artists withdraw from the 2019 Whitney Biennial?

Eight artists removed their work to protest Whitney board vice chairman Warren Kanders, whose company Safariland manufactures tear gas used against migrants at the US-Mexico border. Kanders resigned from the board on July 25, 2019.

What role did Decolonize This Place play in the Whitney protests?

Decolonize This Place organized eight consecutive weekly protest actions inside the Whitney beginning in early 2019. The collective had been running actions at the museum since 2018 and was central to coordinating and sustaining pressure on the institution.

Did the Whitney Biennial 2019 controversy change how museums handle donor ethics?

Not formally, and not immediately. But several mid-size institutions quietly reviewed board affiliations in the months following Kanders' resignation. It accelerated internal conversations about donor transparency that were already starting across the sector.
