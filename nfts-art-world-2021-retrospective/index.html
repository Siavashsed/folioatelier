NFTs Hit the Art World in 2021 and Nothing Has Been Quite the Same Since

A 2021 retrospective on how NFTs reshaped arts collectives, artist income, and the gallery system. What actually changed, and what didn't.

What the Beeple Moment Actually Did for Collectives

The reflexive take in early 2021 was that NFTs democratized art sales. No galleries, no middlemen, artists keeping a larger cut of revenue. And in some cases that was true. Platforms like Foundation and SuperRare saw application numbers spike dramatically in the weeks after the Christie's sale. NonFungible.com's 2021 Yearly Report tracked total NFT art market sales volume reaching roughly $2.6 billion across that year, up from under $100 million in 2020. That's not a rounding error. That's a structural shift in where money was moving.

But here's the thing... collectives didn't benefit evenly, and the platforms themselves became a new kind of gatekeeper almost immediately. Getting accepted to mint on the most visible platforms required existing social capital. If you already had 50,000 Twitter followers and connections to crypto-native collectors, you were in. If you were a printmaking collective in Baltimore with a ten-year exhibition history and no crypto wallet, you were watching from outside.

I've seen this pattern before in the art world. A new distribution channel opens, the people already closest to money and visibility rush in first, and the promise of broader access gets deferred. NFTs in 2021 followed that curve almost perfectly, even while genuinely creating new income for some artists who had never sold through traditional galleries at all.

The Royalty Structure That Actually Mattered

The most consequential feature of NFTs for working artists wasn't the headline sales. It was the smart contract royalty, which let creators earn a percentage, typically between 5% and 15%, every time their work resold on secondary markets. This was a structural change that the traditional art market had resisted for decades. In most jurisdictions, once you sold a painting, you saw nothing from its appreciation. The artist resale right existed in parts of Europe under the EU Resale Right Directive, but it was notoriously difficult to enforce and limited in scope. DACS (Design and Artists Copyright Society) reported that in 2020, UK artists collected just over £1.9 million through resale royalties combined, a number that sounds significant until you compare it to total auction house revenues.

The NFT royalty model, in theory, automated what the traditional market had failed to deliver. And for certain artists, particularly digital artists who had never had a seat at the secondary market table, it created income that simply hadn't existed before. Collectives that minted together and built shared royalty structures started experimenting with models where secondary sales funded communal studio space or shared equipment.

The common misconception here is that royalty enforcement was automatic and permanent. It wasn't. By late 2021, debates were already surfacing about whether marketplaces could or would honor royalties set on other platforms. The technical promise and the business reality were already starting to separate, which became a much bigger issue in 2022. In 2021 though, most people were still optimistic enough to build on the assumption that the smart contract would hold.

Collectives That Got It Right and What They Did Differently

The collectives that navigated 2021 best weren't necessarily the ones that minted the most or generated the biggest headlines. They were the ones that used the moment to formalize internal agreements before going public. My friend Terrance, who runs a multi-disciplinary collective in Detroit, told me that their first NFT drop nearly collapsed the whole group because they'd never actually written down how revenue would be split. Honestly, watching them figure this out in real time over a group chat thread at 1am was one of the more chaotic things I witnessed that year, and I say that as someone who once moderated a collective meeting that ended with two people throwing granola bars at each other over attribution credit.

The collectives that thrived treated NFTs as an organizing tool as much as a sales channel. They minted collectively, shared wallets, and used the process to write governance structures they'd been avoiding for years. Groups like Fingerprints DAO emerged in 2021 as models for how collector and artist communities could formalize around shared ownership of works. The DAO structure, borrowed from crypto culture, gave some collectives a vocabulary for decision-making that felt more equitable than the usual informal hierarchies.

I'm not 100% sure this works for everyone, but the pattern I kept seeing was that collectives with at least some prior experience running cooperative financial structures adapted faster. If you'd already figured out how to split grant money fairly, you had a head start on splitting wallet proceeds.

The Environmental Debate That Divided Studios

You couldn't write about NFTs and arts collectives in 2021 without addressing the carbon conversation, and I'm not going to skip it here. The proof-of-work blockchain infrastructure underpinning Ethereum, the dominant platform for NFT art, carried a significant energy cost per transaction. The Guardian reported in March 2021 that a single NFT transaction on Ethereum at that time could carry a carbon footprint equivalent to a transatlantic flight, though that figure was disputed by crypto advocates who argued the methodology was flawed and didn't account for renewable energy use in mining.

For collectives with explicit environmental commitments, this was not an abstract debate. It was a real values conflict. Some groups publicly refused to mint on proof-of-work chains. Others switched to Tezos, which used proof-of-stake and claimed a fraction of the energy cost. The platform hic et nunc on Tezos became a genuine alternative community space, rougher and less financially spectacular than Ethereum platforms but aligned with a broader ethic that mattered to a lot of collective artists.

The honest position in 2021 was that both sides were overstating their case. The energy cost was real and significant. The claim that NFTs were categorically destroying the planet was also exaggerated. What was true was that artists and collectives were suddenly being asked to make infrastructure decisions with environmental and political weight, and most art school curricula had not prepared anyone for that. The learning curve was steep and the social pressure was immediate.

If you're part of a collective thinking about how 2021's NFT moment applies to your work right now, the most practical thing you can do is write your internal agreement before you do anything public. Not the minting, not the wallet setup. The agreement. Who decides what gets released, how revenue splits, what happens if someone leaves. The technology moved fast in 2021 and exposed every informal assumption that collectives had been running on. The groups that came out of that year intact weren't necessarily the richest or the most famous. They were the ones that had done the boring governance work first.
