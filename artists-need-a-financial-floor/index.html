Artists Don't Need a Budget. They Need a Floor.

Practical finance tips for people with lumpy, unpredictable income, from a writer who spent years getting this wrong before getting it right.

If your income arrives in unpredictable lumps, the single most useful thing you can do is stop budgeting by month and start paying yourself a fixed salary from a buffer account. Everything else is secondary. I learned this the hard way in 2019, when a gallery paid me £4,800 for a catalogue essay in one transfer and I felt genuinely wealthy for about eleven days. By March I was checking my banking app at 6am like it might have changed overnight. The money hadn't vanished on anything dramatic. It just leaked.

Most finance advice assumes a salary landing on the 28th. It assumes your February looks like your March. For anyone in arts, collectives, freelance production, or any commission-driven work, that assumption quietly breaks every rule built on top of it. So the advice arrives sounding sensible and lands useless. What follows is what actually held up across roughly seven years of irregular pay, two bad tax years, and one very awkward conversation with an accountant named Priya who did not sugarcoat things.

Pay Yourself a Salary, Even If Nobody Else Will

Here's the mechanic. Every payment you receive goes into one account you never spend from. Call it the holding account. On the 1st of each month, you transfer a fixed amount to your spending account. That fixed amount is your salary. You set it low, deliberately low, based on your worst quarter from the last two years rather than your best.

When I first tried this I set the number too high because I wanted to feel successful. That lasted four months. The second version, set about 30% lower, has held since. The uncomfortable part is that a good month no longer feels good. You get £6,000 and you still pay yourself £2,200 and it feels like a punishment. It isn't. It's the whole point.

The buffer target most people should aim for is six months of that salary figure, not six months of expenses. Those are different numbers and the second one is bigger and scarier and stops people from starting. Start with one month. The Federal Reserve's Survey of Household Economics and Decisionmaking has found for years that a meaningful share of adults cannot cover a $400 emergency with cash. One month of buffer already puts you well past that line.

Automate the Boring Part Because Willpower Is a Terrible Asset

The strongest evidence in personal finance isn't about picking investments. It's about defaults. Vanguard's How America Saves reporting has consistently shown plan participation rates above 90% under automatic enrollment versus far lower rates when people must opt in themselves. Same people. Same money. Different default.

So build defaults. Standing order on the 2nd into a pension or ISA or whatever the equivalent is where you live. Separate account for tax, funded the day money lands, at whatever rate your accountant tells you plus five points. I keep 32% aside and I have never once regretted the extra cushion in January.

Things I'd automate first, in order:

  • Tax set-aside, same day as every payment
  • Retirement contribution, fixed and small enough that a bad quarter won't force you to cancel it
  • Buffer top-up, whatever is left after the salary transfer

I've written before about the difference between habits that survive a bad month and habits that only work when you're motivated, and this is the cleanest example of it. Automation survives your moods.

The Advice That Doesn't Work for Everyone

Now the honest caveat. The debt avalanche method, paying highest interest first, is mathematically correct and I still recommend it to most people. But my friend Tomas tried it for eight months with three cards and just... stopped. Gave up entirely. He switched to the snowball, killed the smallest balance in six weeks, felt something click, and cleared everything within two years. Cost him maybe £340 extra in interest. Honestly? I'm not sure the optimal method is the right method for everyone, and I say that as someone who spent an embarrassing amount of a dinner party in 2022 arguing the opposite at a man who turned out to be an actual economist. He was polite about it. I was not.

Similarly, the standard line that you should always max tax-advantaged accounts before anything else assumes stable income. If a locked-up account leaves you with no accessible cash, one slow quarter puts you on a credit card at 24% APR. Liquidity has value that a spreadsheet won't show you.

Where Creative Work Makes Money Behave Differently

Arts income has a shape most calculators can't model: long gaps, occasional windfalls, and payment terms that are frequently ignored. In my work with small collectives I've seen invoices sit at 90 days as a matter of routine, and the fix is almost never legal action. It's structural. Deposit up front, 40% minimum, non-refundable, stated plainly. That one change did more for my cash flow than any expense cut.

Track your gross annual figure, not your monthly one. Monthly comparison is psychologically brutal in this line of work and tells you almost nothing. Look at rolling twelve-month totals instead. And when you're deciding where money goes long-term, the case for cheap broad index funds over active picking remains hard to argue with. S&P's SPIVA scorecards have shown for well over a decade that the large majority of active managers trail their benchmark over ten and fifteen year windows. Your edge is in your craft, not in stock selection. I've written more on which conventional money advice holds up and which quietly falls apart.

Open a second current account today, name it something you won't touch, and set every future client payment to land there instead of your spending account. That's it. That single redirection is the thing that makes the salary system possible, and it takes about nine minutes in most banking apps. You can figure out the salary number this weekend.

Frequently asked questions

How much should I pay myself if my income changes every month?

Take your worst three-month stretch from the past two years, divide by three, and start there. It will feel too low. That's the correct feeling. Raise it only after your buffer covers six months of that figure.

Should I pay off debt or build savings first?

Build one month of buffer first, then attack debt above roughly 8% interest, then return to the buffer. Without any cash cushion, the next unexpected bill goes straight back on the card and you never actually get ahead.

What percentage should freelancers set aside for tax?

Ask your accountant for your effective rate, then add five percentage points on top. I hold 32% and it has covered every bill with room left over. Overshooting costs you nothing except a slightly smaller spending account.
