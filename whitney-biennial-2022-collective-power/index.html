How the Whitney Biennial 2022 Forced a Real Conversation About Collective Power in American Art

The Whitney Biennial 2022 wasn't just an exhibition. It was a referendum on who gets to define American art and who funds it.

Why the Collective Model Dominated the 2022 Biennial

Of the 63 artists and groups in Quiet as It's Kept, a striking number operated through some form of collaborative or community-based practice. Groups like Postcommodity, the art collective spanning Indigenous and Chicanx traditions, appeared alongside individual artists whose work was explicitly rooted in community authorship. This wasn't curatorial tokenism. Edwards had been vocal in interviews about her interest in how communities produce meaning together, and the exhibition's structure reflected that. Works weren't just about collectives. They were made by them.

The broader data supports why this moment mattered. According to a Grantmakers in the Arts report, funding for artist collectives and collaborative practices had been growing steadily since 2018, but institutional exhibition space remained disproportionately skewed toward individual artists. The 2022 Biennial began to correct that imbalance, at least symbolically, in one of the most visible exhibition spaces in the country. And symbols matter more than people admit in this field.

I've seen this pattern play out in smaller regional biennials too. In my work covering collective-based practices over the past several years, the funding infrastructure was always lagging behind the actual creative output. Organizations were making extraordinary work with almost no institutional recognition. The Whitney putting collectives at the center of its 2022 edition sent a signal that was felt well beyond Manhattan.

The Sackler Controversy Didn't Disappear, It Evolved

You can't talk about the Whitney in 2022 without addressing the ongoing fallout from the opioid crisis and arts philanthropy. The Whitney had already removed the Sackler name from its spaces in 2021, following years of pressure from activists, particularly the group P.A.I.N. (Prescription Addiction Intervention Now) led by artist Nan Goldin. But 2022 was the year that conversation matured from protest to policy. Museums across the country were being asked not just to return dirty money, but to build new ethical funding frameworks from scratch.

This is where it gets genuinely complicated, and honestly where I think a lot of the arts press got it wrong. The conversation kept getting framed as 'should museums take money from bad people,' which is a real question but also a distraction from the harder structural one: what does sustainable, ethical collective funding actually look like? The Art Forum coverage at the time was thorough on the surface-level drama but thin on the institutional alternatives being proposed.

A 2022 Americans for the Arts report estimated that nonprofit arts organizations in the U.S. lost more than $4.5 billion in revenue between 2020 and 2022 due to pandemic closures. That context is essential. Museums weren't just weighing ethics in the abstract. They were weighing ethics against survival. And collectives, which tend to run leaner and with more community accountability, were often better positioned to weather that pressure with their integrity intact.

What the Biennial Got Right and What It Still Got Wrong

Okay, I'll be honest here. I went into opening weekend slightly skeptical, mostly because a friend of mine, Yara, who runs a small print collective in Bed-Stuy, had tried to submit work through the open call process in 2021 and got absolutely nowhere. She described the process as 'designed to feel accessible while actually being very closed,' and I laughed at the time but then felt kind of bad about laughing because she wasn't wrong. The Biennial's collective-friendly curatorial vision and its actual acquisition and selection infrastructure were not always moving in the same direction. That gap is real and it doesn't get talked about enough.

That said, the work itself was often extraordinary. Simone Leigh, who had just represented the United States at the Venice Biennale (the first Black woman to do so), brought a gravity to the show that elevated every piece around it. Her focus on Black femme interiority and the architectures of care gave the exhibition an emotional spine. But the curatorial framing around collective practice sometimes flattened the differences between collectives that are genuinely horizontal in structure and those that are effectively one artist with a group name. That's a real distinction, and conflating the two can actually undermine the case for collective models.

The common misconception in coverage of this Biennial was that the turn toward collective practice was primarily political signaling. It wasn't. Or it wasn't only that. Artists working collaboratively are responding to material conditions: rising studio costs, the impossibility of solo careers in many cities, and the genuine creative advantages of shared knowledge. According to the National Endowment for the Arts' 2022 report on artists and cultural workers, median earnings for individual fine artists in the U.S. sit around $50,000, with enormous regional variance. Collective structures, in many cases, redistribute that precarity into something more survivable.

What This Moment Means for Arts Collectives Going Forward

Here's what I'd argue, and I'm willing to plant a flag on this: 2022 was the year the American art institution stopped treating collective practice as a subgenre and started treating it as a primary mode. That shift is irreversible. The Whitney Biennial carries enormous cultural weight, and when it organizes itself around collaborative and community-based work, other institutions follow. We've already seen that in the programming shifts at the New Museum, at the ICA in Los Angeles, and in several major regional biennials in the months after the Whitney's run.

But there's a version of this that goes badly... where institutions absorb the language of collective practice without changing the actual power structures underneath. A collective that wins a residency at a prestigious museum but has no control over how their work is framed, documented, or monetized hasn't gained much. The 2022 Biennial gestured toward a more equitable model without fully delivering one. And the collectives I've spoken with since then are clear-eyed about that. They celebrated the visibility while staying cautious about what visibility costs.

So what should you actually take from this? If you're part of a collective or thinking about building one, the 2022 Biennial's legacy is this: institutional recognition is available, but it arrives with conditions. The collectives that came out of 2022 with the most agency were the ones that had built their own audiences, their own documentation practices, and their own funding relationships before they needed the institution's approval. That's not cynicism. That's just what I've seen work, at least in this field, over and over again.

If you take one thing from the 2022 Biennial's moment: build your collective's public record now, before you need institutional access. Document your process, publish your methodology, and grow an audience that belongs to you. Institutions are more likely to come to you on better terms when you already have proof of life that exists outside their walls.
