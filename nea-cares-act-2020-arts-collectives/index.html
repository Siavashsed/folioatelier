How the NEA's 2020 CARES Act Funding Became a Lifeline and a Lightning Rod for Arts Collectives

The NEA's $75M CARES Act distribution in 2020 reshaped arts collectives overnight. Here's what it meant, who it helped, and what it left out.

What the NEA Actually Did With $75 Million, and How Fast

The speed of the distribution was, honestly, impressive. The NEA moved the $75 million through its existing state arts agency network, pushing funds out to all 50 states, six U.S. jurisdictions, and regional arts organizations within weeks of the CARES Act passing on March 27, 2020. For an agency with an annual budget of around $162 million at the time, this was a substantial emergency injection. The logic was defensible: use infrastructure that already existed rather than build new systems during a crisis. State arts agencies could get money to local organizations faster than a centralized federal application process would.

In practice, that meant organizations with existing relationships with their state arts agencies moved quickly and got funded. Groups that had been applying for state grants for years knew exactly who to call. Larger institutions with development staff were ready to write proposals within days. The organizations most likely to receive relief were the ones least likely to be on the verge of collapse. I've seen this pattern repeat across every major arts funding cycle I've covered: the application-ready organizations absorb the emergency funds, while the most precarious spaces struggle to document their eligibility fast enough to matter.

According to NEA research, arts and cultural production contributes over $877 billion to U.S. GDP annually, a figure from their 2017 data that makes the $75 million figure look even more modest in context. The money helped. But it was not remotely proportional to the scale of the collapse.

Who Fell Through the Cracks, and Why That Was Predictable

Artist collectives, particularly those organized as informal associations, worker cooperatives, or fiscally sponsored projects, faced a wall of eligibility questions. Many CARES Act arts funds required recipients to be incorporated nonprofits with a track record of state or federal grant receipts. Collectives that operated through fiscal sponsorship arrangements, where a larger nonprofit holds funds on behalf of a project, found that the rules varied wildly by state. Some state agencies allowed it. Others didn't. And nobody had clear answers in the first two weeks, which is exactly when the decisions were being made.

This wasn't just a paperwork problem. It reflected a real ideological divide about what counts as a legitimate arts organization. Collectives that center community participation, mutual aid, and non-hierarchical decision-making often resist the corporate nonprofit structure precisely because that structure imposes governance requirements that conflict with their values. Asking them to suddenly produce board minutes and audited financials to access emergency funds felt, to a lot of people I spoke with at the time, like a test designed for them to fail.

The virtual pivot that many collectives attempted in spring 2020 added another layer of complexity. As I wrote about separately in my coverage of how the sudden shift to virtual art fairs reshaped the collectives scene, moving programming online wasn't just a technical challenge. It changed audience relationships, revenue models, and what it even meant to be a collective in any meaningful sense. Groups that couldn't access CARES funding were simultaneously being asked to reinvent their entire operation with zero capital.

The Reactions That Came From Inside the Field

Okay, I'll be honest: this is the part of the story where my notes from 2020 get a little chaotic, because the reactions from within the arts world were coming from every direction at once. My friend Terrence, who runs a queer arts collective in Philadelphia, texted me sometime in late April saying they'd finally gotten confirmation of a small state grant, and his message was basically 'we got $3,000, I cried in the kitchen, still don't know if we survive the summer.' Specific and slightly absurd and completely real. I think about that text whenever I hear anyone describe the CARES Act arts funding as a success story.

Broader field reactions broke roughly into three camps. First, gratitude: many established nonprofits publicly acknowledged the NEA's speed and credited the funding with keeping staff employed through the spring. Second, critique: advocacy organizations including Americans for the Arts pushed hard for expanded eligibility and more funding, eventually advocating for what would become additional relief discussions in subsequent legislation. Third, and most interesting to me, a growing call for systemic redesign. A number of collective-led organizations began publishing position papers and open letters arguing that the crisis had exposed a structural bias in American arts funding that predated COVID by decades.

The debate that emerged was genuinely productive, even if the immediate funding gap was not resolved. It pushed state arts agencies to examine their own eligibility criteria, and several revised their fiscal sponsorship policies before the end of 2020. That's not nothing. But it also shouldn't have taken a pandemic to get there.

What This Moment Actually Meant for Collectives Going Forward

Here's the thing... the CARES Act funding moment in 2020 was not primarily a story about money. It was a stress test of assumptions. It revealed that the infrastructure American arts philanthropy had built over 50 years was designed to preserve a particular kind of institution, and that when crisis hit, those were the institutions that got protected first. Collectives that knew this intellectually suddenly had a live case study to point to.

The longer-term effect has been a shift in how many collectives think about their relationship to institutional funding altogether. Some doubled down on mutual aid networks and community fundraising, deliberately reducing their dependence on grants they might not qualify for anyway. Others moved toward formal incorporation, a pragmatic choice that's worth taking seriously even if it involves real trade-offs in terms of governance and autonomy. I'm not 100 percent sure either path works for every collective, because the right structure genuinely depends on what a collective is trying to do and who it serves.

By the time the arts world started thinking about what accountability in collective spaces really looked like, the questions raised in 2020 were already shaping bigger conversations. The discussions that later surfaced at events like the Whitney Biennial 2022 around collective power in American art were seeded, at least in part, by the frustrations and organizing that happened during this exact funding crisis. The CARES Act moment didn't resolve anything. But it named something that the field had been reluctant to name clearly: that structural inequity in arts funding is not a side effect of the system. It is the system, until enough people decide to build a different one.

According to a 2020 report from the Urban Institute, organizations with budgets under $100,000, which describes a significant proportion of artist collectives, were the least likely to receive COVID-19 relief funding despite being among the most financially vulnerable. That single data point should have been a policy alarm. In most cases, it wasn't treated as one.

If you're part of a collective or artist-run space right now, the most practical thing you can take from the 2020 CARES Act moment is this: document your organization's structure, your fiscal relationships, and your grant history before the next crisis arrives. Not because that paperwork reflects your value, but because the funding systems that exist right now are built around it, and being unprepared in a fast-moving emergency means watching the window close before you can get through it. Push for policy change in the long run. But protect your ability to access whatever imperfect systems exist in the short run.
