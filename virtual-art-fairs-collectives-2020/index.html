How the Sudden Pivot to Virtual Art Fairs in 2020 Reshaped the Collectives Scene Forever

A 2020 retrospective on how virtual art fairs and online collectives responded to COVID-19, and what it meant for the future of arts communities.

Art Basel's Online Viewing Rooms and What They Actually Proved

When Art Basel launched its Online Viewing Rooms for the cancelled Hong Kong edition in March 2020, the platform hosted around 235 galleries from 35 countries. The numbers were widely covered, but the reaction from smaller collectives was split almost immediately. On one hand, the platform worked. The Art Newspaper reported that Art Basel's online fair attracted over 250,000 unique visitors in its first week, a figure that dwarfed the physical fair's typical attendance. On the other hand, the same galleries that dominate the physical fair dominated the digital one. Pace. Gagosian. Hauser and Wirth. The top tier didn't change.

What did change was visibility for a specific category of participant: mid-tier galleries representing artist collectives that had never made the physical cut. In my work with several artist-run spaces over the years, I'd seen the fair circuit function as a self-reinforcing loop. You needed the sales record to get the booth. You needed the booth to build the sales record. The online format, because it cost dramatically less to participate, let some of those collectives break that loop, at least partially. It wasn't perfect access. But it was more access than before.

The common misconception at the time was that virtual fairs would permanently democratize the market. They didn't, not fully. The galleries with the biggest marketing budgets still drove the most traffic to their viewing room pages. But the infrastructure created in those first frantic months of 2020 didn't disappear when the world reopened.

Collectives Organizing Online While Institutions Struggled

Here's the part of the 2020 story that didn't get enough coverage at the time: while major institutions were paralyzed, many artist collectives moved faster than anyone expected. Groups like Discwoman in New York and the Diaspora Pavilion network in the UK had been running decentralized, community-first models for years. When the physical world closed, they already had the organizing muscle to shift online without losing momentum. Discwoman moved its programming entirely to streaming platforms within weeks. The Diaspora Pavilion launched new digital commissions specifically because its overhead wasn't tied to a physical space.

This wasn't luck. It reflected something real about how collectives operate differently from institutions. A collective's primary asset is its community, not its building. Americans for the Arts published data in mid-2020 showing that arts organizations with annual budgets under $1 million reported slightly better adaptive capacity during the shutdown than larger institutions, largely because they had less fixed infrastructure to maintain. That finding surprised a lot of people in the sector. It shouldn't have.

The genuine debate that emerged was whether this agility was sustainable or whether it was just a short-term advantage born of having nothing to lose. I'd argue it was both, and that the collectives that used 2020 to build actual digital audiences rather than just digital substitutes for physical events came out of that year with something durable.

The Tools That Stuck and the Ones That Didn't

Okay, I'll be honest about something that feels a little embarrassing in retrospect. In April 2020, a friend of mine, a ceramicist who runs a small collective in Bristol, convinced me that AltSpace VR was going to be the future of gallery openings. We both spent an entire Sunday afternoon trying to set up a virtual gallery walk for her collective's spring show. It took four hours, the avatars looked like they were designed in 2009, and exactly eleven people attended, three of whom were her family. We were absolutely convinced we were on the cutting edge of something. We were not. AltSpace was a dead end for visual art, at least at that stage of the technology.

What actually stuck were simpler tools. Zoom studio visits became a genuine practice. Not a replacement for in-person work, but a real format in its own right. Curators I spoke with throughout 2020 described scheduling 15 to 20 studio visits a week that would have been logistically impossible in a pre-pandemic calendar. Artists in cities that rarely attracted international attention, Tbilisi, Bogota, Lagos, started appearing in conversations at a frequency that reflected genuine expanded access, not just tokenism. ARTnews documented this shift extensively, noting that several major curators publicly credited video calls with expanding their geographic scope of research during 2020.

The tools that failed were the ones trying to replicate the feeling of a physical opening rather than build something native to digital space. The ones that succeeded treated the screen as its own medium. That distinction matters, and it's one the field is still working through.

What 2020 Actually Changed for Artist Collectives Long-Term

The most lasting structural change from 2020 wasn't a platform or a policy. It was a shift in how collectives thought about their own audience geography. Before March 2020, a collective based in Glasgow or Guadalajara largely built its audience within its city and whatever festival circuit it could afford to attend. After March 2020, geography became genuinely more porous. That porousness created new opportunities and new pressures simultaneously.

The pressure worth naming honestly: digital visibility doesn't automatically translate into economic sustainability. Many collectives that built impressive online audiences in 2020 still struggled to convert that visibility into sales, commissions, or institutional support. I'm not 100% sure this works for everyone, but the collectives I watched come through 2020 strongest were the ones that used digital reach to build a mailing list, a direct relationship with supporters, rather than just a social media following. That distinction is less glamorous but more durable.

The opportunity that's still underexplored is cross-collective programming. When geography stopped being a barrier, collectives that had been working in parallel without knowing each other started connecting. Some of those connections produced genuinely new collaborative formats. A 2020 report from Culture Case on arts participation noted that digital formats increased collaborative programming between organizations by a measurable margin during the shutdown period. Whether that collaboration survives the return of physical events is the question the field should be asking right now, in late 2020, rather than waiting to find out.

If you're part of a collective that spent 2020 building online programming, don't let that infrastructure go dormant as physical events return. The mailing list you built, the studio visit format you refined, the cross-city relationships you started: these are concrete assets. Use them to design at least one program in 2021 that couldn't have existed without the digital infrastructure 2020 forced you to build. That's not nostalgia for a terrible year. That's using a hard year's output for something worth keeping.
